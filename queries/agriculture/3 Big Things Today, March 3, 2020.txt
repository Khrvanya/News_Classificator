Soybeans, Grains Higher Overnight, Weekly Export Inspections of Corn Decline.
Soybeans and grains were higher in overnight trading on some bargain hunting and on speculation that central banks are making moves to curb the effect of the coronavirus spread.
Governments are widely expected to take measures including cutting interest rates in a big to ease the impact of the coronavirus, or COVID-19.
The CME Group’s FedWatch Tool puts the odds of a 50-point rate cut later this month at 100%. Equity markets in the U.S. are pointing toward a positive open as Dow, S&P 500, and Nasdaq futures are all higher on the speculation.
The World Bank and International Monetary Fund said in a joint statement yesterday that they stand ready to help offset the shock of the spreading disease.
The death toll in the U.S. now stands at six, which has sparked panic buying at retailers such as Costco, Target, and Walmart. There are now more than 100 cases in the U.S. in 15 states.
Soybean futures for May delivery rose 5¢ to $9.06 a bushel overnight on the Chicago Board of Trade. Soy meal added 10¢ to $309 a short ton, and soy oil gained 0.46¢ to 29.37¢ a pound.
Corn futures rose 4¾¢ to $3.80¼ a bushel overnight.
Wheat futures for May delivery gained 1¾¢ to $5.25 a bushel, while Kansas City futures added 3¼¢ to $4.61 a bushel.
Export inspections of corn for overseas delivery were lower week to week, while soybean and wheat assessments increased, according to the USDA.
Corn inspections in the seven days that ended on February 27 were reported at 896,221 metric tons, the USDA said.
That’s down from 912,922 tons a week earlier, but slightly ahead of the 865,617 tons examined during the same time frame last year.
Soybean assessments, meanwhile, were up to 670,608 metric tons from 596,274 tons a week earlier but down from the 848,895 tons inspected during the same week in 2019, the agency said in a report.
Wheat inspections came in at 654,097 metric tons last week, up from 436,937 tons the previous week and 488,829 tons during the same week last year.
Since the start of the marketing year on September 1, corn assessments now stand at 14.1 million metric tons, well behind the 25.8 million tons inspected during the same time frame the previous year.
Soybean inspections since the start of September, however, rose to 29.6 million metric tons from 26 million tons at this point last year, the USDA said.
Wheat assessments since the start of the grain’s marketing year on June 1 are at 18.8 million metric tons, up from the 17 million tons inspected during the same period last year.
A so-called hydrologic outlook is in effect for pretty much the western half of North Dakota, as melting snow is causing a sudden rise in streams west of the Missouri River, according to the National Weather Service.
Above-normal temperatures that are forecast through much of the week are leading to melting snow, and even in areas where there’s not enough water in the snow to cause flooding, ice jams are presenting a hazard, the NWS said in a report early this morning.
“East of Bismarck, the snowpack will prove to be a little more resilient, but runoff should be in full swing by late in the week,” the agency said in its report.
Flooding is still an issue along the Mississippi River along the Missouri and Arkansas borders with Illinois, Tennessee, and Louisiana, the NWS said.
